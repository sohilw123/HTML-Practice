Deontology

Credit to Pix4Free user Nick Youngson

Deontology is based on the idea that not only outcomes are important for determining whether something is ethical, but avoiding taking certain actions is important too. This philosophy holds there are certain actions we should avoid taking ('bad' actions like killing someone) in any case, and certain actions we should always do ('good' actions like keeping a promise). Deontology presents the idea that we have certain duties and responsibilities that we must uphold. It is from these ideas present in this philosophy that people came up with the concept of human rights. The idea is that it is our duty and responsibility to respect and treat people in a certain way (again, the idea that certain actions should be taken - in this case respecting people and treating them a certain way).

Advantages
  1. This makes use of common sense, it is easy to understand.
  2. It is straightforward and easy to follow. You can intuitively determine right from wrong when evaluating an action. You know whether or not an action will directly harm someone.
  3. Deontology is sensitive to context. If you are using deontology to evaluate in action in different contexts, the evaluation of whether or not the action is ethical will change given the context.
  4. Takes into consideration other people's rights.
Disadvantages
  1. There is no clear list of duties or actions that you should take.
  2. It does not follow a logical step-by-step procedure.
